The advice you'll find most places is to get a small dog if you live in an apartment. That's not wrong exactly, but it's not the full picture either. Some small breeds are energetic, anxious, or vocal in ways that make apartment living genuinely difficult. And some larger breeds are so calm and low-energy that they thrive in smaller spaces.

Here's what actually matters.

## Energy Level Matters More Than Size

A Jack Russell Terrier is small but needs more exercise and mental stimulation than most people expect. A Greyhound is large but famously lazy indoors. They sprint for a few minutes and then sleep for the rest of the day. Many people are surprised to find that ex-racing Greyhounds are among the best apartment dogs for exactly this reason.

What you're looking for in an apartment dog is a lower baseline energy level, a willingness to settle down after exercise, and a temperament that doesn't require constant stimulation.

## Noise Is a Real Consideration

Barking is a problem in apartments in a way it isn't in a house. Some breeds are quiet by nature. Basenjis barely bark at all, and Greyhounds are similarly quiet. Other breeds like Beagles, Shelties, and most terriers were bred specifically to be vocal and alert, which makes them a harder fit for close-quarters living.

## Breeds That Work Well

**French Bulldog** — Compact, quiet, and content with moderate exercise. The most popular apartment dog for good reason. Watch the health issues around breathing.

**Shih Tzu** — Low energy, quiet, and very happy indoors. Needs grooming but not much exercise.

**Pug** — Affectionate and lazy. They overheat easily, which is actually less of an issue indoors.

**Cavalier King Charles Spaniel** — Gentle, adaptable, and not particularly energetic. One of the most apartment-suitable medium-small breeds.

**Greyhound** — Surprisingly good apartment dogs. They need one good run a day and then they're done.

**Basset Hound** — Slow-moving and content to lounge. They can be vocal, but less so than many other hound types.

## What to Think About Before Getting Any Dog

No breed is automatically an apartment dog. Every individual dog has its own personality, and how it was raised and socialised matters as much as breed. A dog that's been taught to settle down and be calm indoors will thrive in an apartment. One that hasn't been taught this will struggle regardless of breed.

Exercise is also negotiable if you're creative. A dog that gets two good walks a day plus some training or puzzle feeders indoors can be well-exercised even without a garden. The breeds above are good starting points. The effort you put in determines whether it actually works.
