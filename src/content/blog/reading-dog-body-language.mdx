Most dog bites could be avoided if people understood what dogs were communicating before things escalated. Dogs rarely bite without warning. The warnings are just subtle, and most people were never taught to read them.

Here's a practical guide to the signals that matter most.

## The Tail

A wagging tail is the most misread signal in dog behaviour. People assume it means happiness, but it really means arousal. The dog is engaged and reacting to something. Whether that reaction is positive or negative depends on the rest of the body.

A loose, wide wag with a relaxed body means a genuinely happy dog. A stiff, fast wag held high means a dog that is alert and potentially tense. A low wag that moves the whole back end is usually a submissive or appeasing dog.

A tucked tail is fear or submission. A tail held straight out horizontal is focused attention. The dog is locked onto something.

## The Ears

Forward-pointing ears mean the dog is alert and engaged. In some breeds this is harder to read because the ears fold or flop, but you can usually see the base of the ear pulling forward.

Ears pressed back flat against the head signal fear, appeasement, or submission. This is different from ears that are simply relaxed and hanging to the sides.

## The Eyes

Soft, relaxed eyes with a slightly squinted look are a content dog. Hard, staring eyes are a sign of tension. The dog is focused intensely on something and is not comfortable.

Watch for what trainers call "whale eye." You can see the whites of the dog's eyes because they've turned their head but are keeping their gaze fixed elsewhere. This is a stress signal.

A dog looking away and blinking is trying to defuse tension. This is called a calming signal and it's the dog saying it is not a threat and wants everyone to calm down.

## The Body

A relaxed dog has a slightly loose posture. A stressed or aroused dog becomes stiffer. Weight shifting forward signals confidence or aggression. Weight shifting back signals uncertainty or fear.

Hackles raised along the back means elevated arousal. It's not always aggression, but it's always worth noticing.

A play bow, front end down, back end up, tail wagging, is an invitation to play and is about as clear a signal as dogs give.

## Putting It Together

No single signal tells the whole story. A wagging tail on a stiff body with hard eyes is a very different dog from a wagging tail on a loose body with soft eyes.

Learning to read the whole picture takes time, but it's worth it. Dogs communicate constantly. Once you start paying attention, you realise they're actually pretty clear about how they're feeling.
