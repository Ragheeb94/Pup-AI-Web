Most dog owners have been there. Someone asks what breed your dog is, you say the name you were told when you got them, and then a vet or a dog trainer gives you a look that says they're not so sure.

The truth is that a lot of dogs look nothing like what their paperwork says. Breeders sometimes mislabel. Shelter workers make educated guesses. And mixed breeds can look wildly different from either parent.

So how do you actually find out?

## The Old Way

Before AI and DNA tests, people relied on physical traits. Ear shape, coat type, body proportions, tail curl. These all give clues. A long nose and a deep chest suggests hound. A double coat and a curled tail points toward spitz-type breeds like Huskies or Samoyeds.

The problem is that physical traits are unreliable in mixed breeds. Two dogs that look almost identical can have completely different ancestry once you test them. Coat colour in particular is nearly useless as a breed indicator because the same colour shows up in dozens of breeds.

## DNA Testing

DNA tests like Embark and Wisdom Panel are the gold standard. You swab the inside of your dog's cheek, mail it in, and get back a breakdown of which breeds are in the mix, sometimes going back three or four generations.

These tests are genuinely useful. They can flag genetic health risks, confirm whether your "Labrador" is actually part Boxer, and explain all those personality quirks you could never quite account for.

The downside is cost and time. Most DNA tests run $80 to $200, and results take two to three weeks.

## AI Breed Identification

AI breed scanners like Pup AI are faster and cheaper. You photograph the dog, and the AI analyses visual features to identify the most likely breed and alternatives.

The accuracy is impressive for purebreds and common mixes. It's less reliable for unusual crosses or dogs with very non-standard coats. Think of it as a strong starting point rather than a definitive answer.

For most people who just want to satisfy their curiosity quickly, an AI scan is the right first step. If you need genetic health information, that's when a DNA test is worth the investment.

## What the Breed Actually Tells You

Here's the thing. Breed is interesting, but it's not everything. Two dogs of the same breed can have completely different personalities depending on how they were raised and socialised.

That said, breed does tell you something useful. A Husky needs very different exercise to a Basset Hound. A Border Collie needs mental stimulation in a way that a Shih Tzu simply does not. Knowing the breed helps you understand what your dog was built for, which is the first step to giving them what they actually need.
